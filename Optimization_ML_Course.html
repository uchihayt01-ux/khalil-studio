Exercise 3 — Norms are convex
Show that both $\|\mathbf w\|_1=\sum_i|w_i|$ and $\|\mathbf w\|_2^2=\sum_i w_i^2$ are convex functions.
$\ell_1$
Each $|w_i|$ is convex (V-shape, triangle inequality $|\alpha a+(1-\alpha)b|\le\alpha|a|+(1-\alpha)|b|$). A sum of convex functions is convex $\Rightarrow\|\mathbf w\|_1$ convex.
$\ell_2^2$
$\|\mathbf w\|_2^2=\mathbf w^TI\mathbf w$, a quadratic form with Hessian $2I\succ0$ (all eigenvalues $=2$). Positive definite $\Rightarrow$ strictly convex.
Why it matters: convex losses have a single global minimum, so gradient descent is guaranteed to find it. $\ell_1$ promotes sparsity, $\ell_2$ keeps weights small & smooth.

Parts II–V — Comparing the variants
Batch
Needs the full gradient over all $n$ points $\Rightarrow$ cost $O(n)$ per update. For 1M points one update scans all 1M rows.
SGD
Updates from one sample $\Rightarrow$ $O(1)$ per step, far cheaper than batch's $O(n)$. Noisy but can start improving immediately and escape shallow minima.
Mini
Batch of size $b$: $b=1\Rightarrow$ SGD, $b=n\Rightarrow$ Batch GD. A compromise — vectorised speed with controlled noise.
100M observations: Batch GD is unrealistic (one update scans all 100M). SGD/mini-batch become attractive: cheap streaming updates, no need to fit all data in memory. Mini-batch scales best.

Part 3 — Equality-constrained minimization

Minimize $f$ subject to $h(\mathbf x)=\mathbf a^T\mathbf x-c=0$, with $\mathbf a=(1,1)$, $c=1$.
  1. Write the augmented Lagrangian.
  2. Interpret each term.
$\mathcal L_\rho(\mathbf x,\lambda)=f(\mathbf x)+\lambda\,h(\mathbf x)+\tfrac{\rho}{2}\,h(\mathbf x)^2$
$f$
the original objective we want to minimize.
$\lambda h$
the Lagrange-multiplier term enforcing the equality constraint $h=0$; $\lambda$ prices a unit of constraint violation.
$\tfrac{\rho}{2}h^2$
the quadratic penalty: it smoothly punishes any violation, improving conditioning and convergence over the plain Lagrangian. Multiplier update: $\lambda^{k+1}=\lambda^k+\rho\,h(\mathbf x^k)$.

Bonus (+1 pt) — Optimization in generative AI
How do generative-AI models (e.g. ChatGPT) use optimization techniques — including combinatorial ones — to train neural networks efficiently and quickly?

Training minimizes a loss (e.g. cross-entropy) over billions of parameters by mini-batch stochastic gradient descent with adaptive optimizers like Adam, computing gradients via back-propagation. Speed/efficiency come from techniques such as learning-rate schedules & warm-up, momentum, gradient clipping, mixed-precision arithmetic, and large-scale data/model parallelism. Combinatorial optimization appears in discrete choices — tokenization (e.g. BPE merges), beam/greedy search at decoding, neural architecture search, and hyper-parameter tuning — where the best discrete configuration is sought under a compute budget.

Exercise 1 — Question set (theory)

Q1 — Convexity test
A twice-differentiable 2-D function has Hessian $H=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$ at its stationary point. Is the point a minimum, maximum, or saddle? Justify.

$H_{11}=3>0$ and $\det H=9-1=8>0$ $\Rightarrow$ $H$ positive definite $\Rightarrow$ the point is a local (and, by convexity, global) minimum.

Q2 — Why squared error?
Give two reasons the MSE uses the square of the residuals rather than the absolute value.

(1) It is smooth/differentiable everywhere (unlike $|\cdot|$ at 0), enabling clean gradients. (2) It penalises large errors more heavily and yields a convex quadratic with a closed-form/unique minimum. (Bonus: it matches the Gaussian-noise maximum-likelihood estimate.)

Q3 — Choosing a GD variant
You must train on 80 million rows that do not fit in memory. Which gradient-descent variant do you pick and why?

Mini-batch (or SGD). Batch GD is $O(n)$ per update and needs all data in memory — impossible here. Mini-batch streams small subsets ($O(b)$ per step), gives vectorised speed with controlled noise, and scales to data that doesn't fit in RAM.
